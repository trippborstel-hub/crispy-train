The AI Lifecycle

Where emissions and water occur

Stage 1UPSTREAM • YOUR SCOPE 3 Raw Material Extraction Mining silicon, rare earths, cobalt, copper. Concentrated in a few countries (China refines ~90% of rare earths). You don’t control this.
Stage 2UPSTREAM • YOUR SCOPE 3 Chip Fabrication Energy-intensive (800+ kWh/wafer), water-intensive (5,700 L/wafer), and uses potent greenhouse gases. Concentrated in Taiwan and South Korea.
Stage 3UPSTREAM • YOUR SCOPE 3 Data Centre Construction Concrete, steel, cooling systems. Up to 4,500 t CO₂e per MW — 5× the embodied carbon of an office building. Growing at record pace.
Stage 4UPSTREAM • PROVIDER SCOPE 1+2 Model Training One-time GPU compute event per model version. Absolute training energy growing 4–5×/year — but amortised across all users.
Stage 5YOUR USAGE • PROVIDER SCOPE 1+2 Inference (Every Query) Where you have agency. >90% of a model’s lifetime emissions. This is the stage you directly influence through model choice, task type, and prompt design.
Stage 6DOWNSTREAM • BEYOND CURRENT SCOPING System-Level Effects What does AI use enable or prevent? Induced and avoided emissions. Largest in magnitude, least studied. No “Scope 4” exists.

GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) — the processor chips that power AI. They run thousands of calculations simultaneously, which is what AI models need. This is a whole-life carbon assessment — the same logic as embodied vs. operational carbon in buildings. Stage 6 extends to consequential LCA: what does AI use enable or prevent?

Source: NVIDIA HGX H100 Product Carbon Footprint, 2025. B200 generation: 24% lower carbon per exaflop. Each generation ~3× more carbon-efficient.

Memory (HBM)

The single largest contributor at 42%. HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) stacks 8–12 individual silicon chips (“dies”) on top of each other, connected by tiny holes drilled through the silicon. Each die requires a full manufacturing cycle. A defect in any layer wastes the entire stack — making it extraordinarily carbon-intensive to produce.

NVIDIA HGX H100 PCF, 2025

Chip Fabrication

800+ kWh per wafer, only 28–65 usable chips per wafer. Fluorinated process gases (SF₆, NF₃) have GWPs of 16,000–23,500× CO₂ and account for 80–90% of direct fab emissions.

ASML (2023); IPCC AR6; semiconductor industry data

Data Centre Build

Up to 4,500 t CO₂e per MW. Concrete = up to 80% of building carbon. Scope 3 is 38–69% of lifetime emissions. AWS: 30% reduction using lower-carbon materials. Average site: 224 acres — up 144% since 2022.

Schneider Electric WP99; Amazon 2024 Sustainability Report; WRI

The clean grid paradox: When operations run on 100% renewable energy, embodied emissions dominate the total footprint. BLOOM’s embodied share was 22% — precisely because its French grid was largely nuclear. On a coal grid, the same hardware carbon looks proportionally small. [Luccioni et al., JMLR (2023)]

Water in the AI Lifecycle

The overlooked resource cost — and the tradeoff nobody talks about

The scale

6B gallons

Google DCs (2024). 78% was potable drinking water. Up 8% YoY.

Google 2025 Environmental Report

17B gallons

All US data centres (2023). Projected to double or quadruple by 2028.

Lawrence Berkeley National Lab

5,700 L

Ultrapure water per chip wafer. TSMC: 101B litres in 2023, missed target.

IDTechEx; TSMC (2024)

The water–energy tradeoff

Data centres face a fundamental choice: evaporative cooling (water towers) uses less energy but consumes large volumes of water. Air-side cooling eliminates direct water use but increases electricity demand.

This is not a free choice — it depends on climate, humidity, and local water stress. A facility in Arizona faces a different calculus than one in Ireland.

40% of US data centres are in the most water-stressed areas. Siting decisions lock in this tradeoff for decades.

Where efficiency gains are possible

Direct liquid cooling — coolant piped directly to chips. Eliminates cooling towers entirely. Emerging but not yet standard.

Closed-loop recycling — recapturing and reusing cooling water rather than evaporating it. Microsoft and others piloting zero-water designs.

Non-potable sourcing — using recycled or grey water instead of drinking water. Google: 78% potable today — significant room to improve.

Waste heat recovery — redirecting heat to district heating or agriculture. Common in Nordics, rare elsewhere.

The transparency gap in one number: UC Riverside estimates ~519 mL per 100-word prompt. Google self-reports 0.26 mL per median Gemini query. A 2,000× discrepancy. Until providers report WUE at facility level, water accounting for AI remains guesswork. This is the question sustainability professionals are uniquely positioned to push. [Li et al. (2023); Google (2025)]

“Matched” vs “24/7” renewable energy

Annual matching via PPAs/RECs does not guarantee hourly clean power. A DC can buy wind credits from Texas to “offset” coal in Virginia. 24/7 CFE measures hourly correlation. Google achieves 90% at 5 sites. This distinction applies to your clients’ claims too.

The Jevons Paradox

Google Gemini: 33× more efficient per query in one year. But DC electricity projected to more than double by 2030. Efficiency makes AI cheaper → more adoption → more total demand. Rebound effects: 20–60% for efficiency gains. The risk is system-wide growth outpacing clean supply.

Luccioni, FAccT (2025); energy economics literature

Policy & Regulation

What’s coming — and what sustainability professionals should watch

European Union

EU AI Act — fully applicable August 2026

Providers of General Purpose AI (GPAI) models must document known or estimated energy consumption in technical documentation. Environmental sustainability is a stated core goal. Applies to non-EU providers serving the EU market. Voluntary codes of conduct on environmental performance — to be assessed by August 2028.

EU AI Energy & Emissions Label (consultation open)

European Commission consultation launched to develop a framework for assessing AI energy consumption and carbon footprint. Potential EU-wide energy/emissions label for AI systems — analogous to appliance ratings.

Data Centre Reporting — next deadline May 2026

Data centre operators (≥500 kW IT power) must report annually: energy consumption, water footprint, capacity utilisation, waste heat, and renewable energy usage. Data Centre Energy Efficiency Package expected Q1 2026.

CSRD — delayed but coming

“Stop-the-Clock” delays first reporting to FY2027 (EU companies) / FY2028 (non-EU parents). Once active, will compel detailed environmental performance disclosure including energy and emissions — capturing data centre operators.

United States

California — blocked, but trying again

Big Tech lobbied against data centre disclosure bills in 2025. AB 93 (water disclosure) vetoed by Gov. Newsom. AB 222 (energy/PUE disclosure) stalled. Only SB 57 passed — a study requiring CPUC to assess data centre grid impacts by January 2027. New bills expected in 2026.

SB 253 / SB 261 — indirect capture

California’s broader climate disclosure laws require large companies to report GHG emissions. This indirectly captures data centre operators. No AI-specific federal regulation exists.

Global

China — green AI embedded in Five-Year Plans

Special Action Plan for Green Data Centres (July 2024). 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) confirms green development as a core pillar. AI ambitions explicitly framed within energy transition narrative.

GHG Protocol — no AI module yet

No dedicated methodology for AI emissions accounting. SBTi excludes avoided emissions from targets. The gap will close — organisations building AI emissions accounting now will be ahead when mandatory frameworks arrive.

For sustainability professionals: The regulatory direction is clear — disclosure obligations are expanding, even if timelines slip. The EU AI Act energy documentation requirement (August 2026) is the nearest hard deadline. Organisations that build internal AI emissions accounting before mandates arrive will have a structural advantage.

Sources: EU AI Act (2024); White & Case (2025, 2026); European Commission AI consultation; CalMatters / US News (2026); Mayer Brown (2025); China 14th/15th Five-Year Plans

Provider Disclosure

Five structural barriers to AI emissions accounting

1. No homogeneous unit

There is no agreed-upon unit for measuring AI emissions. Per token? Per query? Per model? Per dollar of spend? Different methodologies yield estimates that vary by 200×. Without a standard unit, comparisons between providers or models are unreliable.

2. Black box models

Anthropic, Google, and most providers disclose no parameter counts, training hardware, or energy data. All comparative research relies on open-weight models. Worse: providers can quietly reduce model capability (“shrinkflation”) or throttle model performance between releases — as seen with Anthropic models ahead of new launches — changing the emissions profile without the user knowing.

3. Training emissions across users

Training a frontier model costs thousands of tonnes of CO₂e. How should this be allocated across millions of users? Per query? Per subscription? Over what time horizon? No methodology exists. Most lifecycle analyses simply exclude amortised training.

4. Embodied carbon from GPUs & data centres

Embodied emissions (chip manufacturing, data centre construction) account for 22–35% of lifecycle impact, yet are almost never reported by providers. Meta omitted them from Llama 2’s published figures. NVIDIA’s H100 PCF (2025) was the first vendor-level disclosure — still not linked to any per-query accounting.

5. Providers lacking transparency

Providers report PUE, renewable %, and Scope 1&2 — but not per-query energy, inference/training split, facility water, or data centre carbon intensity. Google reports a 12% DC emissions reduction via market-based accounting — while actual DC electricity rose 27%. The gap is filled by REC purchasing.

Luccioni et al. (Nature, 2024) proposed mandatory AI energy labels. Ask providers the same questions you would ask any supplier in a Scope 3 assessment. The fact that answers are unavailable is itself a material finding. Procurement is climate strategy. [Luccioni et al., Nature (2024); Watershed (2025)]

Estimating AI Emissions

Three data points that get you most of the way

1 Region

Grid carbon intensity is the single largest variable in AI emissions. The same query on a Norwegian hydro grid (~2 g CO₂/kWh) produces ~200× less carbon than on a coal-heavy grid (~800 g).

Where to find it

Cloud provider region settings. Cross-reference with Electricity Maps for real-time grid carbon intensity by region.

2 Model

Different models require vastly different compute. Claude Haiku uses ~0.22 Wh per query; Claude Opus uses ~4.05 Wh — an 18× difference. Reasoning models (o3, GPT-5) can reach 100× more.

Where to find it

API logs, usage dashboards. Use EcoLogits to estimate per-model inference emissions including embodied fractions.

3 Token Count

Tokens are the best available proxy for how much work the model performed. Output tokens cost more than input tokens — especially for reasoning models that generate extensive internal chain-of-thought.

Where to find it

API responses include token counts. Provider billing dashboards. Claude Code shows token usage per session.

A starting formula: Emissions ≈ tokens × energy-per-token (model-dependent) × grid carbon intensity (region-dependent). This is imperfect — it excludes embodied carbon, cooling overhead, and amortised training — but it captures the two largest variables and is actionable today. [Methodology adapted from Watershed (2025) and EcoLogits documentation]

Tools for estimation

EcoLogits — Python library + web tool for LLM inference emissions (cradle-to-gate LCA)

CodeCarbon — Tracks compute emissions in data science workflows

Electricity Maps — Real-time grid carbon intensity by region

SCI Standard — Green Software Foundation framework for normalised emissions

GreenPT: Sustainable AI in Practice

What it looks like when a provider builds sustainability into the infrastructure

100% Renewable Energy

Infrastructure hosted by Scaleway in France, running on 100% renewable energy. Benefiting from France’s low-carbon grid (~57 g CO₂/kWh, primarily nuclear). PUE of 1.37 (industry average: 1.57). WUE of 0.067 (industry average: 1.8, including legacy facilities; hyperscalers average ~1.3).

Waste Heat Reuse

Servers placed in urban buildings where computation heat is reused for building heating — turning a waste product into a resource. This reduces the net carbon impact of cooling and displaces fossil-fuel heating demand in the host building.

European Data Privacy

Fully GDPR-compliant. All data processed within the EU using self-hosted models — data never leaves their secure infrastructure. ISO 27001 certified. Sustainability and privacy as co-equal design principles, not afterthoughts.

Task-to-Model Routing

A “green router” selects the most efficient model for each request — matching task complexity to compute. Uses compression and quantised model versions to reduce computing power by 20–30% without quality loss. Prompt design and output-token control further minimise energy per interaction.

Real-Time Emissions Tracking

Introduces mWh per 100 tokens as a standardised sustainability metric. Every API request returns real-time CO₂e and kWh data. Carbon intensity calculated dynamically by grid location, time of day, and embodied carbon. Designed to support SCI (Software Carbon Intensity) reporting.

Why this matters: GreenPT demonstrates that sustainable AI infrastructure is not hypothetical — it exists today. Renewable energy, waste heat reuse, intelligent model routing, and per-query emissions transparency are all implementable. The question for participants: are you demanding these capabilities from your current providers?

Sources: greenpt.ai/sustainability; GreenPT API documentation (docs.greenpt.ai); Scaleway infrastructure disclosures

Your AI Footprint: Calculation

Full methodology — two scenarios, assumptions, and what we don’t know

A: Pro plan, heavy Sonnet use, US grid

150 queries/day × 1.0 Wh × 365 = ~55 kWh/yr
55 kWh × 386 g/kWh (US avg, EPA eGRID) = ~21 kg CO₂e/yr → ~29 kg with lifecycle uplift

~45 msgs/5hr rolling window, continuous use. Sonnet at 1.0 Wh midpoint (Ritchie 2025; EcoLogits). Pro rate limits cap throughput. Anthropic does not publish fixed token caps.

B: Max 20×, heavy Sonnet use, US grid

(150 × 5) to (150 × 10) queries/day × 1.0 Wh × 365 = ~274–548 kWh/yr
× 386 g/kWh = ~105–210 kg CO₂e/yr → ~147–294 kg with lifecycle uplift

“20×” is per-session headroom, not 20× daily queries. Weekly caps limit sustained throughput to ~5–10× Pro. The range reflects this uncertainty. $200/mo plan.

C: Max 20×, Opus, US grid

Fewer queries than B (tighter Opus weekly caps) but ~4–8× energy each
Estimated range: ~340–670 kWh/yr × 386 g/kWh = ~130–260 kg CO₂e/yr → ~182–364 kg with lifecycle uplift

Opus uses ~4–8× more energy per query than Sonnet (larger model, more parameters activated per token). But Anthropic applies tighter weekly Opus caps on Max, partially offsetting the higher per-query cost with lower volume. $200/mo plan.

D: Uncapped API, Opus (measured)

1,300 Wh/day (measured median) × 365 = ~475 kWh/yr
475 kWh × 386 g/kWh = ~183 kg CO₂e/yr → ~256 kg with lifecycle uplift

Why the ~180× range (floor to ceiling)?

• Opus activates far more parameters per token than Sonnet — ~4–8× more energy per query

• Claude Code is orders of magnitude more compute-intensive than chat (large system prompts, tool calls, multi-turn agent loops)

• Plan tier determines how many queries you can run before rate-limiting kicks in

• Couch’s API usage = ~4,400 “typical queries” worth of compute per day, uncapped

Full lifecycle uplift methodology

• Embodied hardware (22–35%): GPU manufacturing (HBM memory, processor chips), server assembly, data centre construction. Source: Luccioni et al. BLOOM study (22% on clean grid); Google TPU LCA (~30% market-based)

• Amortised training: Training Sonnet/Opus cost an unknown amount of CO₂e, shared across all users over the model’s lifetime. No accepted allocation method exists. Contributes an estimated 5–15% additional

• Combined uplift: ~40% (midpoint of 30–50% range). Applied as a multiplier to inference-only figures

• Still excluded: network transmission energy, user-side device energy (both likely <5% of total)

Key uncertainties

• Anthropic does not publish per-query energy data for any model

• Pro/Max use dynamic rolling limits, not fixed caps — adjusted during peak hours (confirmed Mar 2026)

• Opus has tighter weekly caps than Sonnet on Max plans

• Scenarios B & C are extrapolated from plan multipliers, not measured

• Scenario D is one user’s measured experience, not a benchmark
